The Railroader's Measure

A vanished quote, a friend's memory, and what it means to do your share.

By Nate

This week's quote came from an unexpected place—and finding its source turned into a small detective story.

"The mark of a man was to be able to say at the end, 'I've done my share.'" — Alfred E. Perlman, 1940

The Story Behind It

A friend posted this quote on Facebook recently, attributed to Alfred E. Perlman. I'd never heard of Perlman, but the quote struck me—and my friend has an impeccable memory for these things. I wanted to use it, but first I needed to verify it.

I searched. Nothing. No book, no speech transcript, no newspaper article. The quote didn't appear anywhere online with a traceable source. For all the internet knows, these words don't exist.

This is the quiet problem with quotations today. It's not that misattributed quotes spread too easily—it's that real quotes, from real people, can vanish entirely if they were never digitized. The internet's memory is vast but shallow.

The Search

I turned to Claude, Anthropic's AI assistant, to help track it down. We searched extensively—quote databases, newspaper archives, books. We found plenty about Perlman: he was a railroad man, and not just any railroad man. Born in 1902 in St. Paul, Minnesota, he started as an engine wiper and car cleaner in the Northern Pacific yards at age 16. He worked his way through MIT, then Harvard Business School, and rose to become one of the most influential railroad executives of the twentieth century.

He ran the New York Central during its glory years, transforming what he called "Vanderbilt's paradise lost" into a lean, aggressive operation. He introduced 70-mph Flexi-Vans to fight off truckers. He replaced cherished four-track mains with reverse-signaled CTC double track. He was, by all accounts, brilliant, demanding, and occasionally abrasive.

But the quote itself? We couldn't verify it. It had all the hallmarks of an orphan quote—words that circulate with a name attached but no traceable origin.

Back to the Source

So I went back to my friend and asked where he'd encountered it. His answer was specific: a one-page memorial for Perlman published in Trains Magazine in 1983, a few months after Perlman's death. The author was David P. Morgan, the legendary editor who ran the magazine for over three decades—and a longtime friend of Perlman's. My friend had owned forty years of Trains before donating his collection, and he remembered the quote verbatim.

Armed with this lead, I tracked down the July 1983 issue, page 4. And there it was, in the final lines of Morgan's eulogy:

"Al Perlman once observed in 1940 that the mark of a man was to be able to say at the end, 'I've done my share.'

He clearly did his.—D.P.M."

1940

The date surprised me. In 1940, Perlman was 37 or 38 years old, working as chief engineer at the Denver and Rio Grande Western—a railroad struggling in receivership. He was years away from his greatest achievements, decades away from his complicated tenure at Penn Central, a lifetime away from the quiet end in San Francisco.

Yet already, at age 37, he had articulated what mattered to him. Not wealth, not fame, not even success in the conventional sense. Just the ability to look back and say: I did my share.

Why It Matters Today

The quote is deceptively simple. It doesn't ask whether you achieved greatness or left a legacy. It doesn't measure you against others. It asks only whether you contributed what was yours to contribute.

There's humility in that framing—and also clarity. Your share isn't someone else's share. It's calibrated to your circumstances, your capabilities, your moment in time. The track laborer and the railroad president both have a share to do. The measure is the same for both.

Perlman lived another 43 years after saying those words. He modernized the New York Central, endured the Penn Central disaster, and then—at 67, when most would have retired in defeat—took over the ailing Western Pacific and nursed it back to health in 90 days.

Morgan was right. He clearly did his.

A Note on Finding Things

There's a lesson here about how knowledge survives. An AI can search the entire indexed internet in seconds and come up empty. A man who read a magazine forty years ago remembers the quote, the author, and roughly which issue. Human memory—fragile, fallible, unsearchable—preserved what the digital world had lost.

The quote existed in print, in a niche magazine, written by one friend about another. It was never transcribed into a database or quoted in a book that Google scanned. Without my friend's memory pointing to the source, these words would have remained unverifiable—and I never would have used them.

Sometimes the only path to the truth runs through someone who was paying attention.
