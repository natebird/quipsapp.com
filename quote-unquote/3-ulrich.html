The Feminist Anthem That Was a Footnote About Funerals

Not Marilyn Monroe — a Harvard historian, writing about Puritan funeral sermons, who meant the opposite of the tote bag.

By Nate

It's on tote bags, mugs, T-shirts, and about a million Instagram captions. Underneath, you'll usually find Marilyn Monroe. Or Eleanor Roosevelt. Or, more recently, Beyoncé. A rallying cry: go break the rules, cause some trouble, be unforgettable.

The real author is a Harvard historian who is very much alive, wrote the line in a footnote-heavy academic journal, and meant almost the exact opposite of what the mug says.

"Well-behaved women seldom make history." — Laurel Thatcher Ulrich, 1976

The Story Behind It

The line comes from a 1976 scholarly article by Laurel Thatcher Ulrich, then an early-career historian, titled "Vertuous Women Found: New England Ministerial Literature, 1668–1735," published in the journal American Quarterly. The subject was Puritan funeral sermons—quiet, pious, dutiful women eulogized for their virtue.

Ulrich's point was an observation about the historical record, not a piece of advice. These well-behaved women, she noted, rarely make history—because history books tend to skip over the women who kept their heads down, did their duty, and caused no scandal. Their good behavior is exactly why we don't hear about them. She was lamenting a gap in the archive, not encouraging anyone to misbehave.

Ulrich, for the record, went on to win a Pulitzer Prize. In 2007 she published a whole book unpacking what happened to her sentence, wryly titled Well-Behaved Women Seldom Make History—because by then the line had traveled so far from her that she had to go reclaim it.

How a Footnote Became a Battle Cry

A journalist quoted the line in the 1990s, and it detached from its context almost immediately. Stripped of the funeral sermons and the argument about archival silence, the words read like a dare: behave badly, make history.

That reversal is the whole story. In Ulrich's original, "make history" means get recorded by historians. In the mug version, "make history" means do something bold and world-changing. Same four words, opposite meaning—and the flip is what made it go viral. A dry academic observation is un-shareable. A permission slip to rebel is a bestseller.

And notice who the words got handed to: Monroe, Roosevelt, Beyoncé—famous women who "made history" in the new sense. The misattribution isn't random. The quote drifted toward names that fit the meaning it had acquired, not the meaning it started with.

Why It Matters Today

Most of our editions are about a quote losing its author. This one is about a quote losing its meaning—which is sneakier, because the words never change. You can quote it perfectly, spell every letter right, and still have it mean the reverse of what its author intended.

Context isn't decoration around a quote. Sometimes it's the difference between a sentence and its opposite.

"Well-behaved women seldom make history" is a lament about who gets forgotten or a call to be unforgettable, and the only way to know which is to know it came from a 1976 essay about Puritan funerals.

The good news: the real story is more interesting than the tote bag. A young historian noticing that goodness is invisible in the archive—that's a sharper, sadder, truer thought than "go misbehave." Worth keeping the version that actually happened.

The Source

Laurel Thatcher Ulrich, "Vertuous Women Found: New England Ministerial Literature, 1668–1735," American Quarterly, Vol. 28, No. 1 (Spring 1976). Ulrich later wrote Well-Behaved Women Seldom Make History (Alfred A. Knopf, 2007) about the phrase's afterlife. The line is frequently misattributed to Marilyn Monroe and Eleanor Roosevelt; its popular "go misbehave" meaning inverts Ulrich's original point about women omitted from the historical record.
