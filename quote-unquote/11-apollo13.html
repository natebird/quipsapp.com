"Houston, We Have a Problem" — Off by One Tense

A documented NASA transmission, quietly out-competed by its own dramatization.

By Nate

Some quotes are wrong by a whole author. This one is wrong by a single verb tense—and that tiny change is the whole point.

"Houston, we have a problem."

It's the line everyone knows from the Apollo 13 crisis: an oxygen tank explodes 200,000 miles from Earth, and a calm astronaut radios that immortal understatement back to mission control.

Except that's not quite what was said. And the difference between what was said and what we remember is a small masterclass in how stories get sharpened.

"Okay, Houston, we've had a problem here." — Jack Swigert, Apollo 13, April 13, 1970

The Story Behind It

Pull the NASA transcript and here's the real exchange. Command module pilot Jack Swigert radioed: "Okay, Houston, we've had a problem here." Mission control asked him to repeat it, and commander Jim Lovell confirmed: "Ah, Houston, we've had a problem. We've had a main B bus undervolt."

Two things are different from the version in your head. It was "we've had a problem"—past perfect, not present. And it was said twice, by two different astronauts, not as a single clean one-liner.

The famous version—"Houston, we have a problem"—comes from the 1995 film Apollo 13, with Tom Hanks delivering it as Lovell.

Hollywood Fixed the Grammar on Purpose

This isn't a case of a screenwriter getting sloppy. It's the opposite—a very deliberate edit.

Screenwriter William Broyles Jr. has explained the change directly: the real past-tense phrasing "wasn't as dramatic." "We've had a problem" suggests something that already happened and might be over. "We have a problem" puts the emergency in the present, ongoing, right now—the audience is in it. So the filmmakers shifted one verb to keep the tension alive.

It worked. It worked so well that the movie's line didn't just become more famous than the real one—it overwrote it. Ask people to quote Apollo 13 and almost everyone gives you the screenplay, not the transcript. A documented historical transmission got quietly out-competed by its own dramatization.

It's the same engine we saw with "Luke, I am your father": the version that's easier and punchier to repeat wins, and the accurate one fades. Here you can even see the edit being made, and why.

Why It Matters Today

There's something almost sweet about this misquote—nobody was deceived, no one's credit was stolen. But it's a clean illustration of a force worth respecting: stories drift toward whatever is most repeatable, not whatever is most true. Tenses get tightened. Two speakers become one. Hesitations get trimmed. Each edit is tiny and reasonable, and the sum is a sentence that never actually happened.

That's why, when a quote matters to you, the move is to find the primary source—the transcript, the tape, the original page—rather than the version everyone repeats. The repeated version has been optimized for sharing, and optimization always costs a little accuracy.

The real line is arguably better anyway. "Okay, Houston, we've had a problem here"—that flat, procedural "okay," the calm of a man reporting a catastrophe like a status update—tells you more about who those astronauts were than any polished movie line could.
