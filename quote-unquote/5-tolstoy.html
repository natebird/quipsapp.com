The Most Romantic Line in Anna Karenina Isn't About Anna

The famous sun line belongs to Levin at the ice rink, not Vronsky on the platform.

By Nate

You've probably seen this line. It floats around Goodreads, Pinterest, and every "most romantic sentences in literature" list ever assembled:

"He stepped down, trying not to look long at her, as if she were the sun, yet he saw her, like the sun, even without looking." — Leo Tolstoy, Anna Karenina (Part 1, Chapter 9)

And almost every time it surfaces, you're meant to picture the famous scene—Vronsky catching sight of Anna through the steam of a Moscow train platform, the moment that sets the whole tragic novel in motion.

That's not the scene. It's not even the same couple.

The Story Behind It

The "he" is Konstantin Levin—Tolstoy's rumpled, overthinking landowner—and the "her" is Kitty Shcherbatsky, who has recently turned down his proposal of marriage.

Levin has come to the Zoological Gardens to watch Kitty skate, because he can't help himself. He arrives at four o'clock with his heart pounding, and works very hard to look like a man who just happens to be at a skating rink. Not a hero stepping into a doomed grand passion. A nervous man pretending not to stare at the woman who just said no.

That context changes everything about the line. Read as Vronsky-and-Anna, the sun metaphor is about overwhelming desire, the kind that burns a life down. Read correctly—as Levin at the ice—it's about something quieter: the way love rearranges your peripheral vision. How someone you cannot stop loving becomes a fixed point you're aware of even when you are carefully, deliberately looking somewhere else.

It's less electric. It's also better.

The Wrinkle Worth Knowing: Whose Words Are These?

There's a second trap here, and it's specific to translated books. The wording above is the Constance Garnett / Maude-era rendering—the version most quote sites have hardened around.

But Pevear and Volokhonsky translate the exact same sentence as:

"He stepped down, avoiding any long look at her as one avoids long looks at the sun, but seeing her as one sees the sun, without looking."

Same image. Different music. Not the same sentence.

So "Tolstoy said" is doing some quiet work. Tolstoy wrote in Russian. Every English version of this line is a collaboration between Tolstoy and a translator, and if you're going to keep the line, it's worth knowing which one you're keeping. A citation that says "Tolstoy, Anna Karenina" and stops there is missing half the authorship.

Why It Matters Today

A quote without its source is a sentence with the lights off. You can still tell it's pretty. You can't see what it's doing.

And when the context goes missing, the internet fills the gap with the most familiar story—which is how a line about Levin trying to play it cool at an ice rink gets recast as Vronsky locking eyes with Anna across a train platform. Same book, wrong scene, and a completely different meaning smuggled in underneath.

So when you save a line from a translated work, save three things, not one:

The wording (and note the translator—"trying not to look long at her" and "avoiding any long look at her as one avoids long looks at the sun" are not interchangeable). Where it actually lives: book, chapter, speaker, the scene around it. And why you grabbed it, in one sentence, because future-you will forget.

The quote you keep that carefully is one you can actually use later—in a toast, in an essay, in an argument with yourself at 2 a.m. The one you grabbed off a screenshot is just decoration.

Levin, looking sideways at Kitty across the ice, would understand the difference.
