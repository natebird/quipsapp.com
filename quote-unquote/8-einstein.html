Einstein's Most Quoted Line Came From a Recovery Pamphlet

The "definition of insanity" line isn't physics — it grew up in church-basement meetings and a 1983 novel.

By Nate

You've heard it in a hundred meetings, pep talks, and LinkedIn posts:

"The definition of insanity is doing the same thing over and over and expecting different results."

And it almost always arrives with the same signature: Albert Einstein. The name does a lot of work. Put Einstein under a sentence and it stops being an opinion and becomes a law of nature.

Except Einstein never said it. And the real origin is stranger—and more human—than a chalkboard full of equations.

"The definition of insanity is doing the same thing over and over again and expecting different results." Quote under investigation

The Story Behind It

Einstein died in 1955. This line doesn't appear anywhere in his letters, essays, interviews, or lectures. The Ultimate Quotable Einstein—the exhaustive, authoritative compilation published by Princeton University Press—files it under misattributions.

The earliest solid traces point somewhere Einstein never was: a 1981 pamphlet from Narcotics Anonymous, where the line shows up as recovery wisdom—a warning to people trying to break a cycle of addiction. It surfaces again in Rita Mae Brown's 1983 novel Sudden Death, where a character delivers it as a memorable aphorism.

So the sentence most people credit to the 20th century's most famous physicist actually grew up in church-basement meetings and a work of fiction, roughly 25 years after Einstein was gone. Its natural habitat is recovery and self-help, not relativity.

Why Einstein Gets Everything

Einstein is a magnet. So are Mark Twain, Gandhi, Churchill, and Marilyn Monroe. Orphan quotes—clever lines with no clear parent—drift toward these names because fame reads as credibility. A snappy thought "by Anonymous" gets scrolled past. The same thought "by Einstein" gets printed on a poster.

It's a kind of reverse plagiarism: instead of stealing someone's words, the culture donates words to someone who never wrote them, because the attribution makes the line feel truer. The quote borrows Einstein's authority, and Einstein—who had opinions about a great many things—gets saddled with a folk saying about repetition.

There's even a small irony worth savoring. Einstein's actual genius was doing the same thought experiments over and over, turning them by tiny degrees, until the results did change. The real man was a monument to patient repetition. The fake quote makes him sound like he'd have told you to quit.

Why It Matters Today

A misattribution like this one is mostly harmless—until you notice what it costs. It erases the real lineage. This line came out of people fighting to break destructive cycles; that's a meaningful origin, and "Einstein said it" paves right over it. The anonymous counselor and the novelist who actually gave us the phrase disappear so a physicist can take the credit.

Restoring the source is its own kind of respect. "Einstein said this" is false. "This grew out of addiction-recovery culture in the early 1980s and passed through a Rita Mae Brown novel" is both true and a better story—it tells you what the line is actually for.

The words are easy. The provenance is what keeps them honest.
