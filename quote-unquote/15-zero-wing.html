All Your Base Are Belong to Us

The internet's first great catchphrase was a translation mistake — on purpose-ish.

By Nate

Let me try something. If any of these land in your chest with a little jolt of nostalgia, this one's for you:

"You must construct additional pylons."
"Nuclear launch detected."
"My life for Aiur!"
"Work, work." — "Zug zug." — "Job's done."
"You are not prepared!"

If you spent any hours in front of StarCraft or Warcraft in the late '90s, you didn't just read those—you heard them, in the exact voice, instantly. That's the strange power of a game line. It bypasses memory and goes straight to muscle. Twenty-five years later, "Zug zug" still means something to you.

Most of those lines are exactly what they seem: great writing, delivered well, burned in by repetition. But the most legendary video-game quote of all time isn't great writing. It's a mistake—and the story of how it happened is the best kind of quote mystery.

"All your base are belong to us." — CATS, Zero Wing (Sega Mega Drive, 1991)

The Story Behind It

The line comes from Zero Wing, a side-scrolling shooter made by the Japanese studio Toaplan, first released in arcades in 1989. When the game was ported to the Sega Mega Drive for European release in 1991, someone added an opening cutscene—and translated it from Japanese to English.

That translation is the whole event. The villain, an entity called CATS, is supposed to announce that he's seized all of the player's bases. In fluent English that's something like "All of your bases have been taken over by CATS." What came out instead was:

"Somebody set up us the bomb." … "All your base are belong to us." … "You have no chance to survive make your time."

According to Toaplan's Tatsuya Uemura, the English wasn't done by a professional translator. It was handled by a member of the design team who happened to be in charge of overseas business. One person, working outside their specialty, produced a script so broken it looped all the way back around to poetry.

How a 1991 Typo Became a 2001 Phenomenon

For nearly a decade, this was an obscure oddity known only to people who'd played an unremarkable shooter. Then the early internet found it.

Around 1999–2000, screenshots and an animated GIF of the intro started circulating on gaming forums. In November 2000, a programmer named Jeffrey Ray Roberts turned the phrase into a techno track, "Invasion of the Gabber Robots." Then came a Flash video setting the line to that music over dozens of doctored photos—and in early 2001 it detonated across the entire web.

"All your base are belong to us" became, by most accounts, one of the first true internet memes—the template for everything that came after. People printed it on signs, slipped it into news broadcasts, hid it in software. A garbled line from a forgotten cartridge became a shared inside joke for the entire early internet.

Why It Matters Today

Here's why this belongs in a newsletter about sourcing. We've spent a lot of editions on translation—how the Constance Garnett Tolstoy and the Pevear-Volokhonsky Tolstoy are not the same sentence, how a biographer's paraphrase of Voltaire became "Voltaire's words." "All your base" is the same lesson turned all the way up:

A translator is an author.

One person's choices—professional or not, careful or rushed—become the words millions remember. Change the translator and you change the line. Sometimes you change it into a legend.

And it's a reminder that provenance isn't only for solemn quotes. The stuff that makes you smile has a paper trail too: a studio (Toaplan), a game (Zero Wing), a year (1991), a real human being who typed a sentence on a deadline and accidentally made history. Knowing that doesn't ruin the joke. It's what makes "Zug zug" and "All your base" more than noise—it makes them yours, with a story you can actually tell.

Nostalgia is just a memory you never sourced. Source it, and it lasts.
